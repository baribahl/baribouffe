How to Choose Better Balsamic Vinegar

Reference

Summary

This is a buying guide, not a recipe. The video argues that many supermarket "balsamic" products are either sweet glazes or thin vinegar blends, while a better balsamic should be dense, grape-must-forward, aged, and used sparingly as a finishing ingredient.

Key Points

  • Avoid products where the character comes mainly from glucose syrup, caramel, flavoring, or a very sweet glaze texture.
  • Thin balsamic-style vinegar can contain grape must, but may also be stretched with wine vinegar; expect more acidity and less depth.
  • Better balsamic is visibly thicker and more concentrated.
  • The key ingredient to look for is aged grape must.
  • Long-aged bottles can be expensive, but a younger dense option around 7 years can still be useful.
  • Use good balsamic as a finishing condiment rather than a bulk cooking acid.

Practical Use

  • Check the ingredient list before buying.
  • Use a small amount on foods where the flavor will be noticeable: salads, cheese, vegetables, or simple finished plates.
  • Treat very sweet balsamic glazes as a different product from aged balsamic vinegar.
