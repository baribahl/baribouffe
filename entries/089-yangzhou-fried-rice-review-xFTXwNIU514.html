Yangzhou Fried Rice Review

Reference

Summary

Uncle Roger reviews a highly technical Yangzhou-style fried rice demonstration. The useful takeaway is not a full recipe, but a set of fried-rice standards: small even cuts, separated rice, wok handling, and restraint about what belongs in a specific regional style.

Key Points

  • Yangzhou fried rice is treated as a specific dish, not a generic fried rice category.
  • Ingredients should be chopped to a similar small size.
  • Spring onion, mushroom, chicken, and char siu-style pork appear as acceptable components in the reviewed version.
  • The reviewed chef uses many bowls and side steps, which makes the dish technically impressive but cleanup-heavy.
  • The video distinguishes high-end Yangzhou-style fried rice from generic takeout fried rice.
  • Uncle Roger questions chicken broth in the workflow because fried rice should not become wet or soupy.

Practical Use

  • Use this as a fried-rice evaluation checklist: separated grains, dry enough rice, hot wok, even cuts, and no watery sauce.
  • Do not force this into a home recipe without the original chef's full method.
  • If making Yangzhou-style fried rice, research the permitted ingredient set rather than adding random leftovers.
