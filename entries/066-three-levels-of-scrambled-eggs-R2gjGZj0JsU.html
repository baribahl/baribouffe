Three Levels of Scrambled Eggs

Three scrambled egg techniques: a gentle nonstick-pan scramble, a slow double-boiler scramble, and a fast Cantonese-style wok scramble with a starch slurry for a silky texture.

Reference Use caution

Before You Use This

Use caution

/!\ WARNING: Soft scrambled eggs can remain lightly cooked. Use very fresh or pasteurized eggs for children, pregnant people, older adults, or anyone immunocompromised.

Summary

Three scrambled egg techniques: a gentle nonstick-pan scramble, a slow double-boiler scramble, and a fast Cantonese-style wok scramble with a starch slurry for a silky texture.

Key Points

  • Level 1 uses a nonstick pan, butter, salt, medium-low heat, and constant spatula movement.
  • Level 2 cooks the eggs in a glass bowl set over simmering water, stirring constantly until the eggs become jammy and luxurious.
  • Level 3 seasons the egg mixture more assertively, adds a cornstarch slurry, then cooks it very quickly in a hot wok with clarified butter.
  • The Cantonese-style scramble is built in large folds rather than small curds.
  • The water-bath version is the slowest and most labor-intensive; the wok version is the fastest but needs heat control.

Practical Use

  • Use level 1 for a reliable breakfast method.
  • Use level 2 when texture matters more than speed.
  • Use level 3 when cooking for a larger plate or when you want big custardy folds instead of soft curds.
  • For the starch slurry, start with a very small amount of cornstarch mixed smoothly with cold water before adding it to beaten eggs.
